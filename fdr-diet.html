F.D. Roosevelt

The Dirty Martini & The 7-Cent White House Lunch

In the midst of the Great Depression, Franklin D. Roosevelt didn't just lead a nation—he ate like it. While the world outside the White House gates struggled for every scrap, FDR and Eleanor Roosevelt instituted a policy of "Economic Dining" that became a symbol of the New Deal era.

The Infamous 7-Cent Lunch

Eleanor Roosevelt, famously uninterested in fine dining, pushed for a menu that reflected the hardships of the American people. This resulted in the 7-cent lunch: a rotation of humble dishes like chipped beef on toast, poached eggs on top of corned beef hash, and spaghetti with boiled carrots.

Dirty Martinis & Sunday Eggs

Despite the austerity of the lunch menu, the President had his indulgences. Every evening, he would personally mix "Dirty Martinis" for his guests, and on Sunday nights, the staff was dismissed so FDR could scramble eggs in a silver chafing dish for his inner circle—a moment of domestic normalcy in a time of global chaos.
