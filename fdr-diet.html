F.D. Roosevelt

The Dirty Martini & The 7-Cent White House Lunch

Franklin Delano Roosevelt led the United States through two of the most brutal chapters in modern history — the Great Depression and World War II — and he did it all while waging a quiet, lifelong battle at the dinner table. Behind the fireside chats and the New Deal legislation, a fascinating food story unfolded: a wealthy aristocrat forced to eat prune pudding for the press, a man who secretly craved grilled cheese and jelly donuts, and a legendary amateur bartender whose martinis were notoriously terrible. This is the full story of what FDR ate, drank, and endured in the White House kitchen.

The Famous 7-Cent Lunch

Two weeks into his presidency, with breadlines stretching around city blocks across America, Roosevelt sat down to a very deliberate lunch: hot deviled eggs in tomato sauce, mashed potatoes, and prune pudding. The total cost came to roughly 7.5 cents per person. Eleanor Roosevelt had designed the meal with the Bureau of Home Economics — eggs for iron and protein, potatoes for energy, prunes and tomatoes for vitamins A and C. The message was unmistakable: even the President of the United States would eat like the people. Reporters asked FDR what he thought. "Good," he said — and he had cleaned his plate. Newspapers across the country published the recipes the following morning.

Prune pudding became something of a White House signature under Eleanor's direction, much to her husband's quiet despair. The austerity extended across nearly every meal. Chipped beef on toast — essentially dried salted meat served over a thick white gravy — appeared regularly. So did corned beef hash, boiled vegetables with thin sauces, and bread-and-butter sandwiches as a standard lunch item. The kitchen under housekeeper Henrietta Nesbitt operated on principles of economy and nutrition above all else. Taste was rarely invited to the table.

The Housekeeper Who Drove FDR to Madness

Henrietta Nesbitt became one of the most quietly infamous figures in White House history. Appointed by Eleanor, she ran the kitchen for the full twelve years of FDR's presidency with a firm belief that food existed to sustain, not to please. The results were legendary — in the worst possible way. Senator Hiram Johnson once sat through a dinner of what he described as "indifferent chowder" followed by pre-sliced mutton that had gone cold, served alongside barely palatable peas. When writer Ernest Hemingway visited in 1937, he had been warned in advance. He was still taken aback by what he later called "rainwater soup" and "rubber squab." Experienced Washington guests made a habit of eating before arriving at the White House.

Nesbitt's vegetables were particularly feared. Her insipid sides, watery soups, and overcooked meats became the stuff of political legend. FDR complained regularly, but rarely sent a plate back — he knew the austere image was politically essential. He once famously grumbled that he would trade a New Deal policy for a decent piece of roast beef. One of his genuine favorites, Maryland terrapin soup, was served to him in a version so watery it became a minor diplomatic incident. Nesbitt, unbothered, chalked his complaints up to what she called "food peevishness." Sweetbreads — a polite name for organ meat — appeared on the menu up to six days a week. Broiled kidneys were not uncommon. Gelatin-based "salads" assembled with canned fruit, mayonnaise, and cream cheese appeared with alarming frequency.

What FDR Actually Loved to Eat

Away from Nesbitt's kitchen, FDR's true tastes emerged — and they were a mix of simple American comfort food and genuine luxury. According to Nesbitt herself, the President liked food "he could dig into." His favorite sandwich was the humble grilled cheese. He loved fish chowder with the same enthusiasm he brought to fireside chats. Scrambled eggs, which he personally cooked on Sunday evenings when the household staff had the night off, were a private ritual — he would take over a silver chafing dish in the dining room and cook for his family, one of the rare moments he felt like a private citizen rather than the leader of the free world.

Hot dogs were another genuine love, earning one of the most talked-about meals in White House history when FDR served them to King George VI and Queen Elizabeth of the United Kingdom during their 1939 visit. Instead of a formal French banquet, the President organized a traditional American picnic on the lawn at Hyde Park, paper plates and all. The King reportedly asked for seconds. The moment became a global sensation — a masterclass in diplomatic charm wrapped inside a ballpark staple.

Fruitcake — maligned by nearly everyone who has ever encountered it — was a genuine FDR favorite. So was fish chowder, a nod to his New England roots. When he traveled to Warm Springs, Georgia, to his so-called "Little White House," a retreat he returned to throughout his presidency, his personal cook Daisy Bonner prepared his all-time favorite dish: Country Captain. A slow-cooked curried chicken stew with green peppers, tomatoes, onions, and rice, it was fragrant and deeply flavored — everything Nesbitt's White House kitchen was not. Bonner cooked for Roosevelt in Warm Springs for twenty years. On the morning of April 12, 1945, she timed a cheese soufflé for his lunch. He died of a brain hemorrhage before it came out of the oven.

When no one was watching and expense was no object, FDR's aristocratic upbringing showed. He genuinely enjoyed filet mignon, lobster, oysters, crab, Lake Superior whitefish, and king salmon. He had a documented fondness for caviar. These were the meals of the Hyde Park mansion and the presidential retreat, the tastes that the public never saw and that Eleanor's 7-cent philosophy could never quite extinguish.

Breakfast: Jelly Donuts and Presidential Coffee

FDR's mornings followed a strict and rather charming routine. His favorite breakfast was the Berliner pfannkuchen — a German-style jelly donut, fried golden, filled with jam, and dusted with powdered sugar. He took them on a tray in his room with coffee, one of the few moments of the day that were entirely his own. The coffee was no afterthought: green beans were roasted fresh each morning in the White House kitchen to produce a dark French roast, and Roosevelt insisted on having the coffee-making equipment brought directly to his room so he could brew it himself. For a man who exercised almost no control over what ended up on his dinner plate, these small morning rituals were a form of quiet sovereignty.

The Children's Hour: FDR's Legendary Martini Ritual

Every evening, as the workday wound down, Franklin Roosevelt held what he called "the Children's Hour." It was strictly his. No discussion of politics. No war strategy. His inner circle gathered in the Oval Office study, and the President rolled up his sleeves, retrieved his beloved silver cocktail shaker, and started mixing martinis. He carried a personal "martini kit" on every trip — foreign or domestic — including the silver tray and shaker, both of which are still on display at the FDR Presidential Library in Hyde Park.

His signature was the Dirty Martini: two parts gin, one part vermouth, a splash of olive brine for that signature salty cloudiness, a lemon twist, and an olive. Sometimes he added a few drops of anisette. Other nights, a dash of absinthe. He occasionally swapped gin for Virgin Islands rum, or stirred in fruit juice or a liqueur if the mood struck him. He never measured. His speechwriter Sam Rosenman recalled watching him add ingredients one after another, seemingly at random, varying the ratios every time. The results, by almost universal consensus, were terrible. Even his own grandson, Curtis Roosevelt, described the martinis as "truly awful." His brother-in-law Hall Roosevelt told him bluntly that he didn't know how to make a martini. His secretary Grace Tully quietly dreaded being handed a glass.

None of this stopped FDR. At the Tehran Conference in 1943, he personally prepared a martini for Joseph Stalin at the height of World War II — the President insisting on the lemon zest as the essential element. Stalin reportedly said it was "cold on the stomach" but not entirely unpleasant. Roosevelt had made a cocktail at which the leader of the Soviet Union could not bring himself to give a full endorsement, and he considered it a diplomatic triumph.

When the occasion called for something more drinkable, FDR produced his "Haitian Libation" — dark rum, brown sugar, orange juice, and an egg white, shaken hard in a frosted tumbler. His son Elliott noted that he made it specifically for his female companions at Hyde Park social gatherings "when he wanted them to feel frivolous." Unlike the martinis, this one was reportedly quite good.

The Paradox on the Plate

Franklin Roosevelt's relationship with food was, in many ways, a portrait of his presidency: publicly self-sacrificing, privately indulgent, always political. He ate prune pudding and sweetbreads to show solidarity with a suffering nation. He quietly dreamed of roast beef. He scrambled eggs for his children on Sunday nights and served hot dogs to royalty. He mixed martinis that tasted like turpentine and called the ritual sacred. And somewhere between the 7-cent lunch and the caviar, between the broiled kidneys and the Country Captain chicken simmering in Warm Springs, he shaped a nation. No great leader was ever more poorly fed — or more human for it.
