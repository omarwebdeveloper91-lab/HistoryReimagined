The Dirty Martini & Terrapin Soup: Inside FDR's Kitchen

Franklin D. Roosevelt presided over some of the most turbulent years in American history. While his public image was one of strength and leadership, his private dinner table was a fascinating mix of forced economy and high-society tradition.

The Legendary Dirty Martini

FDR was famously fond of cocktails, specifically his "Dirty Martini." As seen in our latest video, he preferred a very specific ratio: 2 parts Gin, 1 part Vermouth, and a generous splash of olive brine. He often mixed these himself for guests, though he was reportedly a "terrible" bartender who liked to experiment with strange ingredients like absinthe.

Humble Beginnings: Scrambled Eggs

Because of the Great Depression, the White House staff was instructed to serve "economical" meals. This often meant large plates of scrambled eggs for dinner—a simple, high-protein meal that symbolized the President's solidarity with the struggling American public.

Traditional Comforts: Stuffed Peppers & Terrapin Soup

When hosting more formal events, the menu turned to classic American-Aristocratic fare. Green peppers stuffed with rice were a frequent favorite, as was Terrapin (turtle) soup, which was considered a delicacy of the era.

The Indulgence: Buttered Steak

On the rare occasions he indulged, Roosevelt enjoyed a thick steak served with a massive amount of melted butter. This was a far cry from the 7.5 cent lunches his wife Eleanor famously promoted, showing the dual nature of his life as both a "man of the people" and a wealthy New Yorker.
